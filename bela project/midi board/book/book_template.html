We are building a synthesizer in the open, one board at a time, and writing it down as we go. Not a datasheet and not a marketing page — a workshop notebook. Every decision here was made against a real constraint, and every picture is a render of geometry that already exists in CAD.

The instrument is small, opinionated, and honest about its parts. It runs on Linux, makes sound with sub-millisecond latency, and speaks MIDI over the same 3.5 mm jacks the rest of the rig already uses. This first issue introduces the two boards that do the thinking and the listening — then goes deep on the two boards we designed ourselves: the small carrier that gives the machine a mouth and ears for MIDI, and the wide carrier of screens that finally gives it a face.

00
The brain & the ears

Two boards, one sandwich

The heart of the machine is a stack you can hold between two fingers: a tiny Linux computer with an instrument-grade audio board riding on top of it. Everything else we build plugs into this.

Compute · the brain

PocketBeagle 2

TI AM62x · dual Cortex-A53 · Linux

A credit-card-corner-sized single-board computer. It boots Linux from a microSD, exposes two 2×18 headers of GPIO/serial/audio, and is powered over USB-C. It is the smallest thing we could find that still runs a real operating system — so the synth engine is software, editable, and alive.

Audio · the ears

Bela Gem

Analog cape · stereo I/O · Lumberg TRS

Bela is the reason this is an instrument and not a computer that beeps. It bolts onto the PocketBeagle as a cape and delivers audio with round-trip latency under a millisecond — low enough that the machine feels like an analog box. Stereo line in and out land on 3.5 mm jacks along one edge.

Plug one into the other and you get the sandwich: brain on the bottom, ears on top, connected through those two 2×18 headers. It is complete — except that it has no way to talk MIDI. That gap is where our first board comes in.

01
Chapter one · the board we drew

The MIDI carrier

The sandwich can hear and speak audio, but it is deaf and mute to MIDI. So we designed a small carrier that adds an opto-isolated MIDI IN and a MIDI OUT on 3.5 mm TRS jacks — and, because it shares an enclosure with the audio board, we made it line up with the Bela down to the millimetre.

The decisions

Six choices, each against a constraint

A board this small is nothing but decisions. Here are the ones that shaped it.

01

TRS Type-A MIDI

No full-size DIN. MIDI over 3.5 mm TRS (Type-A) keeps the connectors the same size as the audio jacks and the whole thing pocketable.

02

Opto-isolated input

An H11L1 optocoupler on MIDI IN — the input is galvanically isolated, so a badly-grounded controller can't inject noise or hum into the audio path.

03

Ribbon, not a cape

Stacking a third board would make the device tall. Instead it connects by a 6-way ribbon and lies flat beside the sandwich — a thin instrument, not a tower.

04

UART4, deliberately

Serial MIDI needs a free UART. We tap P2.5 / P2.7 — chosen to dodge the Linux console (UART0) and the Bela's audio pins.

05

Jacks that line up

Same Lumberg 1503-03 jack as the Bela, same 10.7 mm pitch, lifted on hex standoffs — so audio and MIDI jacks sit on one flush panel.

06

A soft power button

A side-actuated switch in the same panel row, wired as a soft-power signal back to the Beagle — one button to wake and sleep the machine.

How it connects

One ribbon, four useful wires

The carrier talks to the sandwich through header J3 — a 2×3 right-angle connector — over a plain 6-way IDC ribbon. Only four conductors do real work: +3V3 and GND for power, and UART_RX / UART_TX for the MIDI stream. Those land on four specific pins of the Beagle's P2 header.

Field note · the one real catch

UART4 is only free when the Bela Gem is not fitted — the Gem borrows those same pins for audio. So this serial-ribbon board is right for a standalone-MIDI Beagle; run audio and MIDI on one machine and the MIDI has to go over USB instead. Honest constraints, written down.

Functionality

Two signal paths, kept apart

The board is really two small circuits sharing a rail. The input is protected and isolated; the output is a couple of resistors driving the line. Neither is clever — both are correct.

MIDI IN · isolated
J1 ring → R1 220Ω → opto LED
opto out → R4 470Ω ↥ +3V3
→ UART_RX · D1 guards the LED
MIDI OUT · driven
UART_TX → R2 220Ω → tip
+3V3 → R3 220Ω → ring
sleeve → GND · TRS Type-A

The power button sits off to the side on its own header: a 10 kΩ pull-up and a 100 nF debounce turn a momentary press into a clean active-low signal the Beagle can read as wake / sleep.

Chapter two · the board that gives it a face

The dual-screen carrier

The sandwich can think, listen, and now speak MIDI — but it is faceless. So we drew a fourth board: a wide carrier that mounts two ultra-long AMOLED bars side by side, butted into one continuous ~1280 × 180 strip, and plugs down onto the whole stack. It is the instrument's eyes.

Display · the eyes

2 × T-Display-S3 Long

LILYGO · ESP32-S3 · 640×180 AMOLED

Each bar is a complete little computer — an ESP32-S3 driving its own 640×180 AMOLED. We mount two landscape and butt them at the seam, so the pair reads as a single ~1280×180 ribbon of screen spanning the instrument's face.

The decisions

Six choices, each against a constraint

A screen this wide, on a stack this small, is a negotiation with physics. Here is how it went.

01

Two bars, one screen

No single panel is this long and thin. Two 640×180 AMOLED bars, butted at the seam, become one ~1280×180 strip — a cinematic ribbon for meters, waveforms and menus.

02

Smart satellites

Each panel carries its own ESP32-S3 and renders its own graphics. The carrier only ships them power and a thin serial feed — the Beagle stays free to make sound.

03

PRU-serial, not SPI

With the Bela fitted, every hardware UART and SPI is spoken for. So each panel is driven over a bit-banged serial link on the Beagle's free PRU pins — one PRU per screen.

04

A male header, on the back

The carrier is the top of the stack, so its connectors face down. The PB2 header is male, pins pointing into the sandwich — you can't mate two sockets, so this side grows the pins.

05

Its own 5 V rail

Two AMOLEDs pull 0.4–1 A. That current gets its own screw terminal straight from the PSU — the PB2 header carries only ground and data, so the audio rail stays clean.

06

Held on four brass legs

A 194 mm board can't hang off one connector. Four M2.5 hex standoffs at the corners carry it level above the sandwich; the central header does the wiring, the legs do the holding.

How it connects

One header down, two cables out

The carrier plugs onto the sandwich through a male 2×18 header — the same PocketBeagle footprint, grown as pins that press into the stack. From there, two 1×5 headers (JA, JB) run a short cable to each panel: +5V · GND · TX · RX · RST. A two-pole screw terminal (J_PWR) brings in the dedicated 5 V.

The carrier is also the MIDI board's home. Rather than reach the Beagle's P2 header — buried under the Bela — the MIDI board's 6-way ribbon plugs into J_MIDI on the carrier's underside, and the carrier passes the signal down through the same U1 header on spare Beagle pins. (UART4 belongs to the audio, so MIDI rides a soft-serial link, like the screens.) One board, every connection.

Field note · what sets the height

Push the male header home and the whole instrument stands ≈ 36 mm tall, base to display glass. The floor isn't the connector — it's the Bela's line in/out jacks at 21.8 mm. The carrier can't sit any lower without touching them, so the jacks, not the pins, decide how thin the synth can be.
